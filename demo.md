## 1. What is the formula of Pythagorean theorem

The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right‑angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.  

If the two legs are \(a\) and \(b\), and the hypotenuse is \(c\), the formula is:

\[
a^2 + b^2 = c^2
\]

This theorem is used to find the length of one side when the other two are known.

The Chinese Civil War was a prolonged conflict between the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) and the Nationalist Party (Kuomintang, or KMT) that shaped modern China. Here is a concise overview:

**Background (1920s–1930s)**
- After the fall of the Qing dynasty in 1912, China entered a period of fragmentation and warlord rule.
- The KMT, led by Sun Yat-sen and later Chiang Kai-shek, sought to unify China under a nationalist government.
- The CCP, founded in 1921, initially cooperated with the KMT in the First United Front (1924–1927) to defeat warlords.
- In 1927, Chiang Kai-shek turned against the communists, initiating a bloody purge and starting the first phase of the civil war.

**Major Phases**
1. **First Phase (1927–1937)**
   - The CCP was driven into rural bases, most famously the Jiangxi Soviet.
   - The KMT launched repeated “Encirclement Campaigns” against communist strongholds.
   - In 1934–1935, the CCP undertook the Long March to escape KMT forces, relocating to Yan’an in Shaanxi.

2. **Second United Front (1937–1945)**
   - The Japanese invasion of China forced the KMT and CCP into a temporary alliance to resist Japan during World War II.
   - Both sides continued to build their military and political strength in preparation for renewed conflict.

3. **Resumption and Conclusion (1945–1949)**
   - After Japan’s surrender in 1945, full-scale civil war resumed.
   - The CCP, led by Mao Zedong, gained popular support through land reform and effective guerrilla tactics.
   - The KMT, weakened by corruption, inflation, and military overextension, lost key battles.
   - In 1949, the CCP captured major cities; the KMT retreated to Taiwan.
   - On October 1, 1949, Mao proclaimed the People’s Republic of China on the mainland, while the KMT established the Republic of China government on Taiwan.

**Aftermath**
- The war resulted in the division of China into the PRC (mainland) and the ROC (Taiwan), a situation that continues today.
- It established communist rule on the mainland and led to significant social, economic, and political transformations under Mao.

This conflict remains a central event in modern Chinese history, with lasting political and international implications.

World War I, which began in 1914, is typically explained by historians through a combination of long-term underlying causes and a short-term trigger. For a first-year university student, the main causes are often summarized using the acronym **M.A.I.N.**, along with the immediate catalyst.

### Long-Term Causes (M.A.I.N.)

1.  **Militarism**: In the decades before the war, the major European powers (especially Germany, Britain, France, and Russia) engaged in a massive arms race. They built up large standing armies and navies, developed detailed war plans (like Germany's Schlieffen Plan), and the belief that military power was the best way to ensure national security made war more likely.

2.  **Alliances**: A complex web of treaties divided Europe into two opposing camps:
    *   The **Triple Entente**: France, Russia, and Great Britain.
    *   The **Triple Alliance**: Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy (which later switched sides).
    These alliances were meant to create a balance of power but instead created a situation where a conflict between two countries could quickly drag all the others in. This is often called the "domino effect" or the alliance system.

3.  **Imperialism**: The European powers were competing fiercely to build overseas empires in Africa and Asia. This competition for colonies and resources increased tensions and rivalry, particularly between Germany (a newcomer) and established empires like Britain and France.

4.  **Nationalism**: This was a powerful force in two ways:
    *   **Pride and Rivalry**: Intense national pride and competition between nations, like the rivalry between Germany and France after the Franco-Prussian War (1870-71).
    *   **Desire for Independence**: In multi-ethnic empires like Austria-Hungary and the Ottoman Empire, subject peoples (like Slavs in the Balkans) wanted to form their own nation-states. This "powder keg" of nationalism was especially volatile in the Balkans.

### The Short-Term Trigger: The July Crisis

The spark that ignited these tensions was the **assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand**, the heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, in Sarajevo on June 28, 1914, by a Serbian nationalist.

1.  **Austria-Hungary**, backed by Germany, blamed Serbia and issued a harsh ultimatum.
2.  Serbia, backed by its ally Russia, did not fully comply.
3.  Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia (July 28).
4.  Russia mobilized its army to support Serbia.
5.  Germany, seeing Russian mobilization as a threat, declared war on Russia (August 1) and, following its Schlieffen Plan, invaded neutral Belgium to attack France, Russia's ally (August 3).
6.  Britain, committed to protecting Belgium's neutrality and its ally France, then declared war on Germany (August 4).

Within weeks, the alliance system pulled all the major powers into a war that would become a global conflict.
